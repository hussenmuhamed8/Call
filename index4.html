Method 01

Grammar Translation Method

GTM
Q1In the Grammar Translation Method, what language is primarily used for classroom instruction?
AThe target language (L2) exclusively
BA mix of L1 and L2 in equal proportion
CThe students' native language (L1)
DNon-verbal gestures and visual aids
✓ Answer: C — GTM classes are conducted in the students' L1. Translation from L2 → L1 is central, and no emphasis is placed on using the target language for communication.
GTM
Q2Which skill receives almost exclusive attention in GTM?
ASpeaking and listening
BReading and writing
CListening and writing
DSpeaking and reading
✓ Answer: B — Reading (usually literary texts) is the central skill. Writing through translation exercises is also practised. Oral/aural skills are largely ignored.
GTM
Q3How is grammar taught in GTM?
AInductively — students discover rules from examples
BThrough role-play and communicative tasks
CDeductively — rules are stated then applied
DThrough pattern drills and mimicry
✓ Answer: C — GTM uses a deductive approach: the rule is presented first and students apply it to new examples.
GTM
Q4What is the PRIMARY intellectual justification given for using GTM?
ATo develop communicative competence quickly
BTo form correct habits through repetition
CTo improve the native language and grow intellectually
DTo reduce anxiety about speaking
✓ Answer: C — Studying the grammar of the target language was believed to deepen understanding of the native language grammar and to provide intellectual development (mental exercise).
GTM
Q5How does a GTM teacher respond when a student gives a wrong answer?
AIgnores the error and moves on
BProvides the correct answer directly or selects another student
CEncourages self-correction using a questioning tone
DUses rods and gestures to guide the student
✓ Answer: B — Getting the correct answer is very important in GTM. The teacher gives the right answer or asks another student to supply it. There is no focus on self-correction.
Method 02

The Direct Method

Direct Method
Q6Which statement BEST describes the Direct Method's approach to translation?
ATranslation is used only to explain grammar rules
BTranslation between L1 and L2 is strictly forbidden
CTranslation is used as the main testing method
DTranslation is used in early stages, then abandoned
✓ Answer: B — The native language must never be used in the DM classroom. Meaning is conveyed through demonstrations, objects, and pictures — never through translation.
Direct Method
Q7In the Direct Method, what type of syllabus is used?
AGrammar-based, organized by rules
BSituation or topic-based
CSkill-based (listening first, then speaking)
DVocabulary list-based
✓ Answer: B — The DM syllabus is organized around situations or topics (e.g., "at the market", "at school"), not around linguistic structures.
Direct Method
Q8How does the Direct Method teacher handle student errors?
AGives the correct answer immediately
BIgnores errors completely to maintain fluency
CTries to get students to self-correct using various techniques
DWrites the error on the board and explains the rule
✓ Answer: C — The DM teacher uses techniques such as a questioning intonation, repeating up to the error, or offering a choice between the error and the correct form — all to guide self-correction.
Direct Method
Q9Why did the Direct Method have difficulty succeeding in public schools?
AParents objected to the ban on the native language
BConstraints of budget, class size, time, and teacher background
CStudents found learning without grammar rules too confusing
DIt required expensive audio equipment
✓ Answer: B — The DM required small classes and highly proficient teachers, which made it impractical in most public education settings due to large classes, limited budget, time, and teacher preparation.
Direct Method
Q10Which of the following is NOT a feature of the Direct Method?
AGrammar taught inductively
BPronunciation worked on from the beginning
CVocabulary memorized as isolated word lists
DAll four skills practised from the start
✓ Answer: C — Memorizing isolated word lists is a feature of GTM, NOT the Direct Method. In DM, vocabulary is taught through demonstration and real context.
Method 03

The Audio-Lingual Method

ALM
Q11ALM is grounded in which two theoretical fields?
ACognitive psychology and transformational-generative grammar
BStructural linguistics and behavioral psychology
CHumanistic psychology and contrastive analysis
DSociolinguistics and neurolinguistics
✓ Answer: B — ALM is grounded in structural linguistics (scientific description of language patterns) and behavioral psychology (habit formation through conditioning).
ALM
Q12In a backward build-up drill, where does the teacher START building the sentence?
AThe beginning of the sentence
BThe middle phrase
CThe end of the sentence
DThe subject of the sentence
✓ Answer: C — Starting from the END preserves natural intonation as the sentence expands backward toward the beginning.
ALM
Q13A minimal pair consists of two words that differ in:
AMeaning only
BOnly one sound
CStress pattern only
DTwo or more sounds
✓ Answer: B — A minimal pair differs in exactly one sound: e.g., ship/sheep, live/leave. Used to train perception and production of similar sounds.
ALM
Q14Why is vocabulary kept to a minimum in ALM?
AVocabulary is considered less important than communication
BStudents focus on structural patterns first; vocabulary comes later
CVocabulary lists were thought to cause anxiety
DTeachers in ALM were not trained to teach vocabulary
✓ Answer: B — The main objective is mastery of structural (grammatical) patterns. Students acquire vocabulary afterward, once sound system and patterns are established.
ALM
Q15In a Chain Drill, students:
ARepeat a sentence backward word by word
BAsk and answer questions one by one around the room
CSubstitute cue words into a sentence slot
DTransform sentences from active to passive
✓ Answer: B — In a Chain Drill, conversation circulates around the room as each student asks a question to the next student, who answers and then asks the next person.
ALM
Q16ALM views language learning primarily as:
AA cognitive process of hypothesis testing
BA creative and social activity
CA process of habit formation
DA relaxation-aided memory process
✓ Answer: C — From behavioral psychology, ALM holds that language is learned by forming habits through repetitive stimulus–response–reinforcement cycles.
Method 04

The Silent Way

Silent Way
Q17Who founded the Silent Way?
ACharles A. Curran
BJames Asher
CCaleb Gattegno
DGeorgi Lozanov
✓ Answer: C — Caleb Gattegno developed the Silent Way. It is classified as a humanistic approach with a cognitive theoretical basis.
Silent Way
Q18What does the principle "Teaching is subordinated to learning" mean?
ATeachers should not teach grammar explicitly
BThe teacher's role is secondary; the learner's active process is primary
CStudents should teach each other instead of the teacher
DLearning should happen outside the classroom
✓ Answer: B — The teacher gives only what help is absolutely necessary and steps back. Only the learner can do the learning; teaching should serve, not dominate, the learning process.
Silent Way
Q19How does the Silent Way view student errors?
AErrors must be prevented through intense drilling
BErrors signal failure and require immediate correction
CErrors are inevitable signs of active hypothesis testing
DErrors are ignored; only fluency matters
✓ Answer: C — Errors are welcomed in SW as natural evidence that learners are actively engaging with and testing the rules of the language.
Silent Way
Q20What is the purpose of the Sound-Color Chart in the Silent Way?
ATo decorate the classroom and reduce anxiety
BTo help students associate each sound with a color block
CTo display vocabulary in color-coded categories
DTo show cultural images from target language countries
✓ Answer: B — Each color block on the Sound-Color Chart represents a specific sound in the target language, helping students build syllables and words without repetition from the teacher.
Silent Way
Q21Chomsky criticized ALM by arguing that language acquisition cannot occur through habit formation because:
ADrills are too boring to motivate students
BPeople constantly create and understand utterances they have never heard before
CBehavioral conditioning works only for animals, not humans
DLanguage is mainly a cultural, not a biological, phenomenon
✓ Answer: B — Chomsky argued that if language were only habit, people could only produce sentences they had already practiced. Since people routinely create novel sentences, language must be rule-based, not habit-based.
Method 05

Suggestopedia / Desuggestopedia

Suggestopedia
Q22Who developed Suggestopedia?
ACaleb Gattegno
BCharles Curran
CGeorgi Lozanov
DJames Asher
✓ Answer: C — Bulgarian psychiatrist Georgi Lozanov developed Suggestopedia, based on his research into the power of suggestion in learning.
Suggestopedia
Q23What is the role of music in Suggestopedia?
ATo provide entertainment between lessons
BTo teach songs as grammar examples
CTo create relaxed concentration that aids retention
DTo activate the left hemisphere of the brain
✓ Answer: C — Music with specific rhythms creates a state of "relaxed concentration," putting learners in an optimal state for absorbing and retaining large amounts of material.
Suggestopedia
Q24What does "desuggest" mean in the context of Desuggestopedia?
ATo suggest new vocabulary indirectly
BTo eliminate psychological barriers and self-limiting beliefs
CTo reduce the amount of grammar taught
DTo suggest the use of the native language
✓ Answer: B — "Desuggest" means to remove the negative, self-limiting beliefs students bring to learning (e.g., "I can't learn languages"). Removing these barriers unlocks the learner's full mental capacity.
Suggestopedia
Q25In the First Concert (Receptive Phase 1), the teacher reads the dialogue:
ASilently while students read along
BWith voice matching the rhythm and pitch of classical music
CIn the students' native language for comprehension
DWhile students perform physical actions
✓ Answer: B — In the First Concert, the teacher reads the target-language dialogue with voice pitch and rhythm synchronized to classical music, activating both brain hemispheres simultaneously.
Suggestopedia
Q26What is "peripheral learning" in Suggestopedia?
ALearning vocabulary through word lists on students' desks
BLearning information from posters on walls without consciously focusing on them
CLearning through peripheral vision exercises
DLearning grammar through songs in the background
✓ Answer: B — Peripheral learning holds that students absorb information from grammar posters on classroom walls without consciously focusing on them — the subconscious mind processes them.
Method 06

Community Language Learning

CLL
Q27CLL takes its principles from the Counseling-Learning approach developed by:
ACaleb Gattegno
BCarl Rogers
CCharles A. Curran
DGeorgi Lozanov
✓ Answer: C — Charles A. Curran developed the Counseling-Learning approach, drawing on Carl Rogers' person-centered view of education, which CLL is based upon.
CLL
Q28In a CLL lesson, where does the teacher (counselor) typically sit?
AAt the front of the classroom behind a desk
BInside the student circle
COn the outside of the student circle
DAt the back of the room to observe
✓ Answer: C — Students sit in a circle. The counselor (teacher) moves around the OUTSIDE of the circle, leaning in to whisper target-language translations when students speak in their L1.
CLL
Q29What is "recasting" as used in CLL error correction?
ARewriting the transcript with correct grammar
BRepeating correctly what the student said incorrectly, without drawing attention to the error
CCasting students in new role-play characters
DAsking a different student to give the correct answer
✓ Answer: B — Recasting means the teacher repeats back what the student said, reformulated correctly, in a natural, non-threatening way — without explicitly highlighting the error.
CLL
Q30In the "Human Computer™" technique, who controls the pace and repetitions?
AThe teacher, according to a fixed drill pattern
BThe student, who chooses what to practice and how many times
CThe whole class together
DA computer program that adjusts to student responses
✓ Answer: B — The student selects which part of the transcript to practice, and the teacher (acting as a "computer") repeats it as many times as the student requests, without correcting mispronunciation directly.
Method 07

Total Physical Response

TPR
Q31TPR was developed by:
AGeorgi Lozanov
BJames Asher
CCaleb Gattegno
DCharles Curran
✓ Answer: B — James Asher developed TPR, modeling it on child language acquisition where physical response to commands precedes speech production.
TPR
Q32TPR is primarily an example of which broader approach?
AThe communicative approach
BThe comprehension approach
CThe humanistic approach
DThe cognitive-code approach
✓ Answer: B — TPR belongs to the comprehension approach, which prioritizes listening comprehension and does not require students to speak before they are ready.
TPR
Q33In TPR, when are students typically ready to start speaking?
AFrom the very first lesson
BOnly after completing a full grammar course
CAfter approximately 10-20 hours of instruction
DWhen they have memorized all commands
✓ Answer: C — After about 10–20 hours of comprehension-based instruction, some students naturally become ready to produce speech. Role reversal (students give commands) then begins.
TPR
Q34What is an "Action Sequence" (Operation) in TPR?
AA set of grammar rules taught in order
BA series of connected commands representing a real-life procedure
CA sequence of vocabulary items in a theme
DA pattern drill performed in sequence
✓ Answer: B — An Action Sequence is a chain of connected commands that together form a complete real-life activity, e.g., "Take out a pen. Take out paper. Write a letter. Fold the letter..."
TPR
Q35Why are TPR commands often made humorous?
ATo practise comedy vocabulary
BTo reduce stress and make learning enjoyable
CTo distract students from difficult grammar
DTo follow the DM principle of situational teaching
✓ Answer: B — Humor in commands lowers the affective filter. A relaxed, fun atmosphere reduces anxiety and makes students feel successful, which facilitates learning.
Method 08

Communicative Language Teaching

CLT
Q36According to Morrow, what are the THREE features of truly communicative activities?
AGrammar focus, fluency practice, error correction
BInformation gap, choice, and feedback
CRole-play, authentic materials, pair work
DListening, speaking, and interaction
✓ Answer: B — Morrow's three features: (1) Information Gap — one person knows something the other doesn't; (2) Choice — the speaker decides what/how to say; (3) Feedback — the speaker gets a response showing whether communication succeeded.
CLT
Q37Communicative Competence includes linguistic competence PLUS:
APerfect grammar and extensive vocabulary
BKnowing when, how, and to whom to say things appropriately
CThe ability to translate between languages accurately
DMastery of all four skills equally
✓ Answer: B — Communicative competence goes beyond grammar (linguistic competence). It means knowing what to say, how to say it, and to whom — appropriate use in real social contexts.
CLT
Q38In CLT, "authentic materials" are used because:
AThey are easier to understand than textbook language
BThey expose students to natural language and help transfer learning to the real world
CThey replace the need for a teacher
DThey focus on grammar in context
✓ Answer: B — Real-world materials (menus, newspapers, weather forecasts, etc.) reflect actual language use, helping students bridge the gap between classroom and real communication.
CLT
Q39What does a "Scrambled Sentences" task in CLT teach?
APronunciation of consonant clusters
BCohesion and coherence in discourse
CVocabulary in context
DTransformation of sentence patterns
✓ Answer: B — Students unscramble sentences to restore the original order, developing awareness of how sentences connect (cohesion) and how a text makes overall sense (coherence).
